Employment Law in Slovenia for Expats

12 min read  ·  Slovenia Legal Guide  ·  Updated April 2026

TL;DR — Quick Summary

Working in Slovenia: EU citizens work freely — no permit needed. Non-EU nationals need EU passport (free movement) or single permit for non-EU. Minimum wage: EUR 1,254/month (2024). Standard hours: 40/week. Annual leave: 20 days. Notice period: 15 days–80 days depending on employment duration. Always have an employment contract reviewed by a lawyer before signing.

Your Right to Work in Slovenia

EU, EEA, and Swiss citizens have the automatic right to work in Slovenia under the principle of free movement of workers. No work permit is required, though registration with local authorities may be needed after 90 days.

Non-EU nationals must obtain work authorisation before starting employment in Slovenia. Working without a valid permit is illegal for both the employee and the employer, and can result in deportation and future entry bans.

Work Permit Routes: EU passport (free movement) or single permit for non-EU

The primary route for non-EU nationals seeking employment in Slovenia is the EU passport (free movement) or single permit for non-EU. Key requirements typically include:

  • A valid job offer or employment contract from a Slovenia-registered employer
  • Proof of relevant qualifications or professional experience
  • Meeting a minimum annual salary threshold (varies by permit type)
  • Health insurance valid in Slovenia
  • Clean criminal record (apostilled)

Applications are submitted to the relevant immigration authority — either from your home country (entry visa) or in-country (permit renewal or change). Processing times range from 4–12 weeks depending on the permit type and applicant nationality. An immigration/employment lawyer can significantly improve your application's success rate.

Key Employment Law Rights in Slovenia

Minimum Wage

Slovenia's minimum wage is EUR 1,254/month (2024). Collective bargaining agreements (sector-level agreements between employers' associations and trade unions) may set higher minimum rates for specific industries. As an expat employee, you are entitled to the same minimum wage protections as any national.

Working Hours

The statutory maximum working week in Slovenia is 40 hours. Overtime beyond this must be compensated — either through additional pay (typically 25–50% premium) or time off in lieu. Your employment contract must specify your hours; be wary of contracts that attempt to waive overtime rights.

Annual Leave

Employees in Slovenia are entitled to a minimum of 20 working days of paid annual leave per year. Many collective agreements and individual contracts provide more than the statutory minimum. Public holidays are in addition to annual leave entitlement.

Notice Periods

Notice periods in Slovenia: 15 days–80 days depending on employment duration. These are minimum statutory entitlements — your contract may provide longer notice periods. During a probationary period (typically 1–6 months), shorter notice applies.

Your Employment Contract — What to Check

Always have your employment contract reviewed by a qualified lawyer before signing. Key terms to verify:

  • Job title and description — must match what was discussed; vague descriptions can be used to expand your role without additional compensation
  • Salary and bonus structure — is the bonus discretionary or contractual? What currency and payment method?
  • Working hours and overtime — are there waiver clauses? Are they enforceable under Slovenia law?
  • Probationary period — length and terms; termination rights differ during probation
  • Non-compete clause — enforceability varies significantly by jurisdiction; in some countries these require compensation to be valid
  • Governing law — your contract should be governed by Slovenia law; be wary of contracts that attempt to apply the law of a low-protection jurisdiction
  • Social security contributions — confirm which country's social security system you will contribute to; this has significant pension implications

Dismissal and Redundancy Rights

Employment protection law in Slovenia provides significant rights for employees facing dismissal. Key points:

  • Minimum notice: 15 days–80 days depending on employment duration
  • Reasons for dismissal: dismissal must be for a valid reason (capability, conduct, redundancy). Unfair dismissal claims can be brought before Slovenia labour courts.
  • Redundancy pay: statutory redundancy compensation is typically calculated on length of service and final salary; collective agreements may provide more.
  • Consultation obligations: collective dismissals (large-scale redundancies) require formal consultation with employee representatives.

If you believe you have been unfairly dismissed or your rights have been violated, consult an employment lawyer immediately — strict time limits apply to employment claims in Slovenia.

Social Security and Tax

As an employee in Slovenia, you will contribute to the national social security system, which typically covers: healthcare, unemployment insurance, pension, and disability benefits. Contributions are split between employer and employee, with rates varying by income level and sector.

As an expat, tax residency rules are important: if you spend more than 183 days per year in Slovenia, you will generally be considered a Slovenia tax resident and liable to pay tax on your worldwide income. Your employer should withhold income tax at source. Consult a tax lawyer or accountant to ensure you are not liable to double taxation under the Slovenia tax treaty with your home country.
