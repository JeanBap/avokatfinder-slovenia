Davek na Promet Nepremičnin

Davek na promet nepremičnin is the Slovenian real estate transfer tax of 2%, payable on the sale of used (resale) property and based on the higher of the sale price or the assessed market value.

Slovenia levies a 2% transfer tax on resale property transactions. The tax is formally payable by the seller but is frequently negotiated to be borne by the buyer in practice, or split between the parties. The tax is assessed and collected by the Financial Administration (FURS — Finančna uprava RS).

The davek na promet nepremičnin does not apply to new-build property sold by a VAT-registered developer — in that case, VAT at 9.5% (reduced rate for residential) applies instead. Buyers must determine which tax regime applies before assessing their total acquisition cost.

FURS assesses the transfer tax against the declared purchase price. If FURS considers the declared price below market value, it may revalue the transaction. The parties can appeal against an inflated assessment but must do so within 30 days of the tax decision.

The tax is due within 30 days of the signed transfer contract. Non-payment or late payment attracts interest and penalties. The Zemljiška Knjiga will not process the registration without confirmation that the transfer tax has been paid.

Common Mistake: Assuming the 2% rate applies to new-build purchases in Slovenia. New builds sold by developers are subject to 9.5% VAT, not the 2% transfer tax — the total tax burden is significantly higher for new-build buyers.
Expert Tip: Clarify in the kupoprodajna pogodba which party bears the davek na promet nepremičnin. The Slovenian default is for the seller to pay it, but many transactions contractually shift this obligation to the buyer — know your position before signing.

Budget the 2% davek na promet nepremičnin alongside legal fees (typically 1–1.5% of purchase price) and Land Registry fees when calculating total acquisition costs for a Slovenian resale property.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who pays the real estate transfer tax in Slovenia?

Under Slovenian law, the seller is formally liable for the 2% davek na promet nepremičnin. However, the parties may contractually agree that the buyer bears the cost. The contract should specify this clearly.

What triggers VAT instead of transfer tax in Slovenia?

New-build properties sold within 2 years of completion by a VAT-registered developer are subject to 9.5% VAT (reduced residential rate) rather than the 2% transfer tax. Resale transactions between private individuals always attract the transfer tax.

Can FURS reassess the declared sale price for transfer tax purposes in Slovenia?

Yes. If FURS considers the declared price materially below market value, it can issue a revaluation and charge the 2% tax on the higher assessed value. Buyers and sellers should declare the genuine transaction price to avoid this risk.
