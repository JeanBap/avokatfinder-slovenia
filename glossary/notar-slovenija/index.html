Notar (Notary)

Slovenia Legal Glossary — Plain-English Definitions

Notar (Notary): A notar (notary) in Slovenia is a state-licensed legal professional authorised to authenticate documents, certify signatures, draft and certify contracts, and perform other official legal functions, regulated by the Notary Act (Zakon o notariatu) and supervised by the Notary Chamber of Slovenia (Notarska zbornica Slovenije).

Notaries in Slovenia are regulated by the Notary Act (Zakon o notariatu, ZN) and are supervised by the Notary Chamber of Slovenia (Notarska zbornica Slovenije). The notary is a public official appointed by the state but operating as a self-employed legal professional. Notaries hold a law degree, must pass the notarial examination, and are appointed to a specific notarial district (notarski okraj) by the Minister of Justice. The number of notaries per district is regulated by the Ministry to ensure adequate geographic coverage without oversaturation.

The notary's core function is to provide public authenticity (javna listina) to legal acts and documents. A notarially authenticated document (notarski zapis or solemnizirana listina) has enhanced legal evidentiary value — it is presumed to be authentic and can be used for enforcement directly without a court judgment in certain cases. Documents that require notarial certification in Slovenia include: contracts for the transfer of real property (kupoprodajne pogodbe za nepremičnine), mortgages (hipotekarne listine), company incorporation acts and statutory changes, prenuptial agreements, wills, and certain powers of attorney.

When a client visits a Slovenian notary for a property transaction, the typical process is: the notary checks the Land Register to verify ownership and encumbrances; drafts or reviews the sale contract; verifies the identity of all parties (via passport or ID card); reads the contract aloud and explains its contents; obtains signed declarations of understanding; authenticates the contract with the notarial stamp and signature; and submits the application for Land Register registration. Foreign clients are entitled to an interpreter at the notarial appointment.

Notarial fees in Slovenia are regulated by the Notarial Fee Tariff (Tarifa za notarsko storitev), which sets fees based on the value of the transaction. Fees are progressive — they increase with value but at a decreasing percentage rate. For a residential property purchase of €200,000, notarial fees are typically €600-900 before VAT. The fee covers the notary's preparation and authentication of the document, the Land Register application, and the archive copy. Clients may be charged additional fees for translations, copies, and other ancillary services.

Slovenia has been introducing electronic notarisation (e-notar) for an increasing range of documents, allowing certain notarial acts to be completed remotely via video link with electronic signature. This has been particularly useful for diaspora Slovenians and foreign investors who cannot attend in person. However, real property transfers and mortgages still require physical presence or a notarially certified power of attorney. The Notary Chamber maintains a list of notaries and their availability for electronic services on its website.

Key Facts About Notar (Notary) in Slovenia

  • Notaries in Slovenia are regulated by the Notary Act (ZN) and supervised by the Notary Chamber of Slovenia.
  • Notarial certification is mandatory for real property transfers, mortgages, company formations, and prenuptial agreements.
  • Notarial fees are regulated by a fixed tariff based on transaction value.
  • Foreign clients are entitled to an interpreter at the notarial appointment.
  • Electronic notarisation (e-notar) is available for some document types; property transfers still require physical attendance or power of attorney.
Common Mistake: Foreign buyers in Slovenia sometimes save notarial fees by trying to use a "private" sale agreement without notarial authentication, not realising that an un-notarised property sale contract cannot be registered in the Land Register and therefore the buyer cannot obtain legal title. Without Land Register registration, the buyer has no protection against subsequent encumbrances or a sale to a third party. Notarial certification of property contracts is not optional — it is a legal requirement for valid title transfer.
Expert Tip: When arranging a notarial appointment in Slovenia for a property transaction, contact the notary at least two weeks in advance. Provide a copy of the draft contract, your identity documents, and the Land Register extract in advance so the notary can review and prepare. If you cannot attend in person, arrange a notarially certified power of attorney in your home country (with apostille and certified translation) well in advance of the completion date.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I find a notary in Slovenia?

The Notary Chamber of Slovenia (Notarska zbornica Slovenije) maintains a searchable register of all licensed notaries at www.notar-z.si. You can search by location or name. Any licensed notary can handle any type of notarial act regardless of where you or the property are located, though using a notary near the property is practical for Land Register filings. All notaries charge the same regulated fee for the same type of document.

Can a notary in Slovenia provide legal advice?

Notaries in Slovenia are required to inform parties of the legal consequences of documents they are certifying and must refuse to certify documents that are contrary to law. However, notaries act as impartial public officials — they do not act as the advocate of either party. If you need someone to negotiate on your behalf, advise on the legal risks of a specific transaction, or represent your interests in a dispute, you need a separately appointed lawyer (odvetnik). For complex transactions, use both a notary and your own lawyer.

What is the difference between a notarially authenticated document (notarski zapis) and a notarially solemnised document (solemnizirana listina)?

A notarski zapis is a document fully drafted and authenticated by the notary — the notary prepares the text, reads it to the parties, and certifies it. A solemnizirana listina is a document prepared by the parties (or their lawyers) that the notary then certifies after verifying the parties' identities and confirming their understanding. For real property contracts, a notarski zapis is required. For certain other documents, solemnization is sufficient. Your notary will advise which form is required for your specific transaction.
