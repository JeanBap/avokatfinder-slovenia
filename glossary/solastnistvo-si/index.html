Solastnistvo (Co-ownership)

Solastnistvo is Slovenian co-ownership of real property, where two or more persons each hold an ideal share (idealni delez) of an undivided property. It frequently arises through inheritance and complicates sales without full co-owner consent.

Slovenian property law recognises two forms of collective ownership: solastnistvo (co-ownership in defined shares) and skupna lastnina (joint ownership with undefined shares, typically between spouses). In solastnistvo, each co-owner holds a specified idealni delez (ideal share — a fraction of the whole) without any physical partition of the property.

Co-owners can use the property collectively. Ordinary management decisions require a majority of shares; major decisions (mortgaging, selling the whole property) require unanimous consent. A single dissenting co-owner holding even a small fraction can block a sale of the whole property.

Inheritance is the most common source of solastnistvo in Slovenia. When a property owner dies, their share passes to all heirs in equal proportions unless a will directs otherwise. Over generations, Slovenian properties — particularly older homes and agricultural land — can accumulate many co-owners, some of whom may be unknown or living abroad.

When one co-owner wishes to sell their individual share, the other co-owners have a statutory right of first refusal (predkupna pravica) — they must be offered the share at the same price and conditions before it can be sold externally. This right must be offered in writing and the co-owners have 30 days to respond.

Physical division (delitvena toba) of a co-owned property is possible by court order if the parties cannot agree. Courts can order either physical division of the land (if feasible) or a forced sale with proceeds distributed proportionally.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a Slovenian property where only some co-owners have signed the deed — because others were unknown or unavailable — creates a title defect. Insist on signatures from every registered co-owner or a court order confirming authority to proceed.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Before making an offer, instruct your lawyer to extract the complete ownership sheet (lastniski list) from the zemljiska knjiga and identify all co-owners. Follow up with FURS if inheritance shares are involved but not yet registered.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is skupna lastnina and how does it differ?

Skupna lastnina (joint ownership) is used between spouses — the shares are undefined and the property is treated as an undivided whole belonging equally to both. A spouse's share cannot be sold without the other's consent. Solastnistvo has defined fractions; skupna lastnina does not.

Can a co-owner mortgage their share?

Yes, a co-owner can mortgage their individual idealni delez without the other co-owners' consent. However, lenders rarely accept a fractional mortgage as security — it would be difficult to enforce on only part of a property.

How do I resolve a dispute with a co-owner who refuses to sell?

If negotiation fails, any co-owner can commence a delitvena toba — a court action for partition. The court will attempt physical division; if impractical, it orders a judicial sale of the whole property with proceeds distributed by share.
