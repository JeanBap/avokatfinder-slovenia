Zemljiška knjiga (Land Register)

The official Slovenian land register maintained by the district courts, recording property ownership, mortgages, easements, and all other real property rights.

The zemljiška knjiga (land register or land book) is Slovenia's official registry of real property rights, maintained by local district courts (okrajna sodišča). Every parcel of land, apartment, house, and commercial property in Slovenia has an entry in the land register that records current ownership, mortgages and other charges, easements, and any legal disputes affecting the property. Reviewing the land register extract (izpisek iz zemljiške knjige) before purchasing any Slovenian real estate is an essential due diligence step and a legal requirement for mortgage lenders.

The land register is organised by property identification numbers. Each property has a main identification (ID nepremičnine) and may also have a unique plot number (parcela) or building unit number (del stavbe) for apartments. A full land register extract shows the ownership entry (lastniški vpis), mortgage entries (hipoteke), easement entries (služnosti), and any annotations (plombe) indicating pending registration applications. The presence of a plomba is particularly important—it means someone has already applied for a change that has not yet been fully processed, and this application takes priority over subsequent applications.

Slovenia's online land register (eLandRegister) has made property searches accessible to anyone. You can access it at e-sodstvo.si and search by property ID or address. Searches are free for basic information, with fees applicable for official certified extracts. Official extracts are required for mortgage applications, property sales, and inheritance proceedings. The register is updated in near-real-time as courts process applications, making online searches highly reliable for current status.

Property transactions in Slovenia require registration in the land register to transfer legal ownership. Your notary (notar) prepares the purchase agreement and the registration clause (zemljiškoknjižno dovolilo or intabulacijska klavzula), which is the seller's formal authorisation for the ownership transfer to be registered. Once signed and notarised, the notary submits the registration application electronically to the court. Registration processing in Slovenia typically takes 2 to 4 weeks. Until your name appears in the land register, you are not the legal owner regardless of any payment made.

Unlike Hungary, Slovenia uses notaries (notarji) rather than lawyers (odvetniki) to authenticate property transactions. Both can draft property contracts, but only a notar's authentication enables land register submission. Slovenian notaries are public officials appointed by the state and bear professional responsibility for the transactions they notarise. Their fees are regulated by the Notarial Tariff Act and calculated based on the property value. For a €200,000 property, notarial fees typically range from €500 to €1,200 depending on the complexity of the transaction.

Key Facts

  • Maintained by local district courts (okrajna sodišča)
  • Accessible online at e-sodstvo.si free of charge
  • Plomba indicates a pending registration application with priority
  • Notar authentication required for land register submission
  • Registration typically takes 2-4 weeks after application

⚠ Common Mistake

Buyers often overlook the significance of a plomba (annotation of a pending application) when reviewing the land register. A plomba may indicate that another buyer has already applied to register ownership, or that a bank is registering a new mortgage. If you sign a contract without checking for a plomba and another registration has priority, your purchase could be invalidated or delayed significantly. Always check the register status on the day of signing.

💡 Practical Tip

Request that your notary include a real estate tax clearance certificate (potrdilo o plačani nepremičninski davek) as a condition of closing. Unpaid real estate taxes run with the property and can become your liability as the new owner. Also verify that the seller has paid all homeowners' association (stanovanjska skupnost) charges, as outstanding balances may transfer with ownership in managed apartment buildings.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I check the Slovenian land register for a specific property?

Visit e-sodstvo.si and use the "Javna knjiga" (public registry) search function. You can search by address, property ID number (ID nepremičnine), or parcel number. Basic information is free. For certified official extracts required for transactions, contact your notary or visit the court registry office directly.

What is a plomba and is it a problem?

A plomba is an annotation indicating that a land register application is pending — it has been received by the court but not yet fully processed and entered. It could represent a pending mortgage registration, a pending ownership transfer, or various other applications. If you see a plomba, investigate its nature before proceeding. Your notary should clarify what the pending application involves and whether it affects your purchase.

Do I need a notary or a lawyer for buying property in Slovenia?

You need a notary (notar) to authenticate the purchase contract for land register submission — notarial authentication is mandatory. You may also engage an odvetnik (lawyer) to advise you on legal aspects of the transaction independently of the notary. Especially for complex transactions or if you don't speak Slovenian, having both a notary and an independent lawyer is advisable.
